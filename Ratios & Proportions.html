Ratios and Proportions

Ratios: The Comparison Game

Ratios compare two or more quantities and show their relationship. They can be written using a colon, such as 2:3

A ratio is essentially a relative comparison between numbers. It tells you how much of one thing you have relative to another thing, not the exact total amount in the universe.

How You Write Them

You'll usually see ratios in three different formats meaning the exact same thing

  • With a colon: 2 : 3
  • As the word "to": 2 to 3
  • As a fraction: $\frac{2}{3}$

Proportions: When Ratios Match

Proportions show that two ratios are equal. They are used in real-life situations such as cooking, map scaling, speed calculations, and distance problems

If a ratio is just a single comparison, a proportion is an equation stating that two separate ratios are totally equal to each other

How You Write Them

$$ \frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d} \quad \text{or} \quad a:b = c:d $$ (Read aloud as: "$a$ is to $b$ as $c$ is to $d$.")

The Secret Weapon: Cross-Multiplication

How do you prove two ratios are actually in proportion? You cross-multiply. If you have the proportion: $$ \frac{2}{3} = \frac{4}{6} $$ Multiply the outer diagonals: $2 \times 6 = 12$, and $3 \times 4 = 12$. Because $12 = 12$, the proportion is true and balanced. If they don't match, the proportion is broken.

Where You Use This in Real Life

  • Cooking: If a recipe feeds 2 people using 1 cup of rice ($1:2$), but you are cooking for 6 people (3 times the crowd), you set up a proportion: $\frac{1 \text{ cup}}{2 \text{ people}} = \frac{x \text{ cups}}{6 \text{ people}}$. Cross-multiply to find $x = 3$ cups of rice.
  • Map Scaling: If $1 \text{ inch}$ on a map equals $50 \text{ miles}$ in the real world ($1:50$), and you measure $3.5 \text{ inches}$ between two cities on the paper, your proportion $\frac{1}{50} = \frac{3.5}{x}$ tells you the real distance is $175 \text{ miles}$.
  • Speed Calculations: If your car travels $60 \text{ miles}$ in $1 \text{ hour}$ ($60:1$), how far do you go in $4.5 \text{ hours}$? Set up $\frac{60}{1} = \frac{x}{4.5}$, and you get $270 \text{ miles}$.
