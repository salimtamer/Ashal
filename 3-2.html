3-2: Linear Functions

The "Constant Rate of Change"

What makes a function "linear"? It’s the fact that for every single unit you move to the right on the x-axis, you move the exact same amount up or down on the y-axis.

  • Linear: If you earn $15 every hour, your pay is linear. It doesn't matter if it's the 1st hour or the 100th; you always get $15 more.
  • Non-Linear: If you earn $15 the first hour, but $30 the second, and $60 the third, the "rate of change" is constantly moving. That is not linear.

Note:Linear functions represent predictable, steady progress.

Deconstructing the Equation: f(x) = mx + b

m (The Slope): The "Velocity" of the Graph

Mathematically, \( m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \)

  • Deep insight: Slope is a ratio of comparison. It tells you how much the dependent variable (y) responds to changes in the independent variable (x).
  • If m = 3, it means "For every step I take in x, I take 3 steps up in y."

b (The y-intercept)

The y-intercept is what happens when x = 0.

  • Deep insight: b is your "starting condition." It’s where the function is before you’ve started adding or subtracting anything.
  • In the real world, if you start a savings account with $50, b = 50. Even if you earn $0 interest, you still have your initial $50. $b$ is the "offset" that moves the entire line up or down on the graph.

The Geometry of the Line

Every linear equation is a constraint. It’s a rule that restricts x and y to a specific path.

  • The "Unbroken" Path: Because a linear function has a constant slope, it creates a perfect, unwavering straight line. If the slope were to change even slightly at x=5, the line would "bend," and it would no longer be a linear function.

Putting It All Together

Now that we understand the components of a linear function, let's see how they work together to create the graph of a line.

The "Map" for Your Graph

Think of the equation f(x) = mx + b as a set of step-by-step directions for your pencil:

  • Step 1: Plot the "Starting Point" (b). Always begin by placing a dot on the y-axis at the value of b. This is your anchor; it’s where your graph lives before the slope kicks in.
  • Step 2: Use the Slope (m) to find the next point. Rewrite your slope as a fraction: $\frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}}$.Rise: How far to go up or down.Run: How far to go to the right.
  • Step 3: Connect the dots. Draw a straight line through your starting point and your new point. You’ve just visualized the function!

Example

You’re grinding a new game and you’re already at Level 10 (b=10). You’re absolutely cracked, gaining 3 levels every hour (m=3).

  • Equation: f(x) = 3x + 10
  • The Graph: It’s a literal roadmap of your rank. The y-intercept (10) is where you started, and the steepness of the line (3) is how fast you’re climbing the leaderboard. Want to know your rank in 5 hours? Plug in 5 and stop guessing—you’re hitting Level 25.
